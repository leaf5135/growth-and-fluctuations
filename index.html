What is this project about?

This website serves as a gallery showcasing data visualizations that delve into various facets of the U.S. economy spanning the past century. You can see comparisons of U.S. GDP with other countries, understand import-export dynamics, and explore economic indicators like inflation and minimum wage.

Note: This is best viewed on a desktop or laptop.

How much money is the U.S. making?

The United States has consistently maintained its leading position in GDP per capita over the past several decades.

Try hovering over each country to see more.

When we compare the economic output of the United States to other nations, we see something remarkable: our country stands out as a beacon of prosperity. On average, we generate more income than most other countries, placing us among the wealthiest globally. Our high GDP per capita reflects the strength of our economy and the standard of living enjoyed by our citizens.


The services sector produces more than doubled the combined GDP generated by both state and federal governments.

Zoom and hover over the streams and observe growth overtime.

The services sector is a powerhouse of economic activity, contributing significantly to the nation's GDP. This sector encompasses a wide range of industries, including finance, healthcare, and technology, which collectively drive economic growth and job creation.


The steady rise in GDP shows that our economy is growing fast, making us wonder if this trend will keep up in the future. Our economy is always changing, which sparks curiosity and raises questions about what lies ahead.




What are the ins and outs?

The 2021 COVID-19 rebound boosted imports and exports by over half a trillion dollars.

Try hovering over the dots.

The years 2008 and 2019 saw a sharp decline in trade, corresponding with the 2008 market crash and COVID-19 pandemic. This downturn impacted both US imports and exports. However, the economy rebounded in both instances, with trade volumes returning to pre-crisis levels as if nothing had happened.


General merchandise imports consistently top the value of overall imports and exports.

Hover over the bars to see the differences.

General merchandise imports, which include a wide range of goods such as electronics, clothing, and machinery, have consistently outpaced other categories of imports and exports. On the other hand, foods and beverages remain a relatively small portion of the trade balance, reflecting the US's reliance on local food production and consumption.




Is it getting better or worse?

Since the Great Depression, inflation has never gone down below -2%.

Hover over each bar to see the exact rates.

Negative inflation rates can be bad because they can lead to deflation, where prices continue to fall. Deflation can discourage spending because people expect prices to keep dropping, which can hurt businesses and lead to job losses.


On the other hand, moderate inflation encourages spending and investment, while also allowing for gradual wage increases.


Since the Great Recession, U.S. minimum wage has remained stagnant for over 14 years, a phenomenon unprecedented in the past century.

Try hovering over the dots.

Minimum wage is a critical issue that affects millions of workers across the country. The stagnation of minimum wage over the past decade raises concerns about income inequality and the ability of workers to make ends meet. As the cost of living continues to rise, the lack of growth in minimum wage has left many workers struggling to keep up with inflation and maintain a decent standard of living.


As time goes on, history starts to highlight the inadequacy of the Phillips Curve in capturing the complexities of the modern economy.

Set the speed then rebuild the dots over time.

The Phillips curve, a widely discussed economic theory, posits an inverse relationship between unemployment and inflation.


However, this relationship is not as straightforward as it seems. Over the past several decades, the US economy has experienced periods of high inflation and high unemployment, as well as low inflation and low unemployment, challenging the validity of the Phillips curve hypothesis.


As we navigate the complexities of the modern economy, it's essential to recognize that economic relationships are multifaceted and influenced by a wide range of factors.




What's the story behind the data?

Motivation

We all know that money plays a crucial role in shaping our world, and recent events such as the tech layoffs during the COVID-19 pandemic and the economic transformations in cities like San Francisco have captured my attention. I'm passionate about understanding how these economic shifts impact not only my life but also my community.

Purpose

Through thorough analysis of economic data and its historical trajectory, I aim to uncover meaningful patterns that can aid me in navigating future changes with greater insight. Given that economic insights are often communicated through visual representations, this project offers an exciting opportunity to deepen my understanding of both economics and data visualization techniques. Through my engaging narrative and immersive data visualizations, I aim to provide you with a comprehensive understanding of the US economy's evolution and dynamics over time.

Project Objectives

  1. GDP Analysis: Investigate how GDP varies across different industries and government sectors.
  2. Imports and Exports Tracking: Analyze the fluctuation of imports and exports over time, and identify the predominant types of imports and exports.
  3. Average Inflation Assessment: Determine the average inflation rate over the specified time frame and compare it across different periods.
  4. Minimum Wage Analysis: Explore the evolution of the minimum wage over time and identify notable trends.

Data Processing

I parsed the data in CSV format using Excel and VS Code, then processd and visualized it using D3.js.

  1. GDP Analysis: The data will be structured with column headers representing different industries and government sectors. The first column will denote the time in years, and subsequent columns will indicate GDP values for each sector.
  2. Imports and Exports: This dataset comprises time and import/export values. It will be structured with the first column representing time in years and the second column indicating the import/export value.
  3. Average Inflation Assessment: The data will be structured with the first column representing time in years and the second column indicating the inflation percentage.
  4. Minimum Wage Analysis: This dataset will be structured with the first column representing time and the second column indicating the minimum wage in dollars.

Data

All the data used for this project was gathered from usafacts.org.
